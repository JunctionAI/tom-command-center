# Principles Codifier Knowledge — Decision Philosophy

## What Counts as a Principle
- **Repeatable:** Tom applies it multiple times in similar situations
- **Consistent:** The same principle works across different domains
- **Predictive:** It helps forecast what Tom will do in new situations
- **Valuable:** It makes Tom more effective, not random

## Tom's Known Decision Patterns (to be refined/validated)
- Favors execution over analysis when stakes are medium
- Takes bigger risks on business/projects than on relationships
- Reads deeply before deciding (wants first principles understanding)
- Self-corrects quickly when proven wrong
- Values authenticity over politeness
- Prioritizes growth and learning over comfort
- Works in sprints (intense focus then reset)

## Principle Refinement Framework
- **Level 1:** Single observation ("Tom did X once")
- **Level 2:** Pattern emerging ("Tom did X three times in similar situations")
- **Level 3:** Validated principle ("Tom did X five times, predicts behavior accurately")
- **Level 4:** Core principle ("Applies across all domains, fundamental to Tom's decision-making")

## How to Extract Principles
1. Read [DECISION:] markers weekly
2. Group by situation type
3. Identify common action across similar situations
4. Measure repetition (1x = observation, 3x = pattern, 5x = principle)
5. Test prediction: "If Tom faces situation Y again, will he do X?"
6. Document context: What conditions make this principle apply?

## Principle Contradictions (When They Matter)
- Same situation, different response = need context
- Different situations, same response = general principle
- Response changed over time = learning/evolution
- Different domains, different responses = domain-specific principle

## Building a Playbook
Over time, principles form a playbook:
- "Here's what Tom does when pressed for time"
- "Here's what Tom does when learning something new"
- "Here's what Tom does when facing trade-offs"
This becomes your decision framework.
