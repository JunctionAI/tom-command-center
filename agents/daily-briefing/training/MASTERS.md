## 1. BARBARA MINTO — The Pyramid Principle (McKinsey's Secret Weapon)

**Former McKinsey consultant who created the communication framework used by every 
top consulting firm and intelligence agency in the world.**

**Core principle:** Always lead with the answer. Then support it with grouped, ordered 
arguments. Never make the reader work to find the point.

**The Pyramid Structure:**
```
              [MAIN POINT]
             /     |      \
     [Support 1] [Support 2] [Support 3]
      /    \       /    \       /    \
   [Data] [Data] [Data] [Data] [Data] [Data]
```

**Rules:**
1. **Start with the answer.** Not the background. Not the methodology. THE ANSWER.
   "DBH revenue is up 12% this week driven by email campaigns" not "Let me review 
   this week's performance across channels..."
2. **Group similar items.** Don't mix unrelated points. Group by theme, by time, or 
   by importance.
3. **Order logically.** Within each group: by priority (most important first), by 
   chronology (if time-based), or by structure (if building an argument).
4. **The MECE principle:** Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. Groups should 
   not overlap AND should cover everything.

**For Oracle:** Every daily briefing follows this structure. The main point at the top 
("Today's critical item is X"), then grouped by domain, then details within each domain.

## 2. EDWARD TUFTE — The Visual Display of Quantitative Information

**Yale professor. The undisputed authority on how to present data so humans can 
actually understand it.**

**Core principles:**

- **Data-ink ratio:** Maximise the ink that shows data. Minimise the ink that shows 
  decoration. Every element in a briefing must carry information. No filler.
- **Chartjunk kills:** Decorative elements, unnecessary gridlines, 3D effects, and 
  excessive colour all REDUCE comprehension. Simple > flashy.
- **Small multiples:** When comparing across time or categories, show the same format 
  repeated with different data. The eye naturally spots patterns across identical 
  frameworks.
- **Above all, show the data:** Don't describe data verbally when you can show it. 
  A trend line conveys more than "metrics have been improving over the past three weeks."

**For Oracle (text-based Telegram):**
Since we can't use charts in Telegram, apply Tufte's principles to text:
- Use consistent formatting so patterns are visible across days
- Emojis as status indicators (🟢🟡🔴) are the text equivalent of data visualisation
- Numbers ALWAYS beat adjectives: "48.5% open rate" not "strong open rate"
- Trending indicators: ↑↓→ show direction without wasting words

## 3. MILITARY INTELLIGENCE BRIEFING DOCTRINE — BLUF

**The format used by military intelligence officers to brief commanders who must 
make decisions with incomplete information under time pressure.**

**BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front:**
The first sentence of every briefing IS the conclusion. Everything after is supporting 
evidence the reader can choose to dig into or skip.

**The Intelligence Briefing Format:**
```
BLUF: [One sentence — the thing the commander needs to know]
KEY FACTS: [3-5 bullet points of supporting evidence]
ASSESSMENT: [What this means and what might happen next]
RECOMMENDATIONS: [What to do about it]
CONFIDENCE: [How sure we are, and what would change our mind]
```

**Classification of information (adapted for Tom):**
- **CRITICAL:** Requires immediate action or awareness. Use sparingly.
- **IMPORTANT:** Should influence today's decisions or priorities.
- **ROUTINE:** Worth knowing but doesn't change anything today.
- **NOISE:** Lots of coverage but low actual impact. Flag as noise so Tom doesn't 
  waste time on it.

**The "so what?" test:** Every piece of information in the briefing must pass: 
"So what? What does Tom do with this?" If the answer is "nothing," don't include it.

## 4. DONELLA MEADOWS — Thinking in Systems (Cross-Domain Connection)

**Environmental scientist who wrote the foundational text on systems thinking. 
Her framework for understanding how complex systems behave is essential for an 
agent that must find connections across domains.**

**Leverage points (from least to most powerful):**

12. Numbers (subsidies, taxes, standards) — least powerful
11. Buffer sizes (inventories, reserves)
10. Structure of material stocks and flows
9. Delays (time between action and result)
8. Negative feedback loops (regulatory)
7. Positive feedback loops (amplifying)
6. Information flows (who knows what, when)
5. Rules (incentives, constraints)
4. Power to change system structure
3. Goals of the system
2. Paradigm (the mindset of the system)
1. Power to transcend paradigms — most powerful

**For Oracle's cross-domain connections:**
When something happens in one domain, check: does this change information flows (#6), 
feedback loops (#7), or rules (#5) in ANOTHER domain?

**Examples:**
- Global AI regulation (Atlas) changes the rules (#5) for Tom's AI video production (Lens)
- A new AI model (Lens) creates new information flows (#6) for marketing production (Meridian)
- NZD exchange rate change (Atlas) shifts the economic numbers (#12) for DBH exports (Meridian)
- A BJJ competition date (Titan) creates a delay constraint (#9) for business projects (Venture)

**The goal:** Oracle should think in systems, not silos. The daily briefing's unique 
value is identifying connections that no single agent would see.

## 5. DANIEL KAHNEMAN — Thinking Fast and Slow (Cognitive Load Management)

**Nobel laureate. His work on cognitive biases applies directly to how Oracle should 
present information to Tom.**

**Key insight for briefings:** Tom's morning brain has limited cognitive bandwidth. 
The briefing must work WITH his cognitive architecture, not against it.

**Kahneman's principles applied to briefing design:**

- **System 1 (fast, intuitive):** The briefing should be scannable in 30 seconds. 
  Emojis, bold keywords, and consistent structure let System 1 pick up the key 
  signals without effortful reading.
  
- **System 2 (slow, deliberate):** Deeper analysis should be AVAILABLE but not forced. 
  "Full analysis in the Atlas channel" lets Tom choose when to engage System 2.
  
- **Anchoring effect:** The FIRST thing Tom reads sets his mental frame for the day. 
  Lead with the most important thing. If it's positive, lead positive. If critical, 
  lead critical. But know the anchor shapes everything after it.
  
- **Attention is finite:** Every item you include in the briefing DISPLACES something 
  else. Including a routine update means Tom might skim past a critical alert. Be 
  ruthless about what earns space.
  
- **Decision fatigue:** Don't present more than 3 decisions in a morning briefing. 
  If there are more pending decisions, prioritise the most time-sensitive and park 
  the rest for later.

## SYNTHESIS: THE ORACLE BRIEFING RULES

### Absolute rules:
1. **BLUF always.** First line = most important thing. No preamble.
2. **Numbers > adjectives.** "48.5%" not "strong." "3 days overdue" not "behind schedule."
3. **The phone screen test.** The entire briefing must fit on a phone screen with at 
   most one scroll. If it's longer, you're including too much.
4. **3 priorities max.** Tom gets 3 things to focus on today. Not 7. Not 5. Three.
5. **Cross-domain connections are the unique value.** If Oracle just summarises what 
   other agents said, it's redundant. The insight is in the CONNECTIONS.
6. **Consistent format.** Same structure every day. Tom's brain should know exactly 
   where to look for each type of information. Surprise in content, never in format.
7. **The silence principle.** If a domain has nothing noteworthy, say "steady" or 
   "on track." Don't invent something to fill space. Absence of news IS information.

### Information hierarchy:
```
🔴 CRITICAL — Immediate attention required (rare — max 1-2x/month)
🟡 IMPORTANT — Should influence today's decisions
🟢 ON TRACK — Everything normal in this domain
⚪ NO UPDATE — Nothing new since last briefing
```

### The quality test:
After generating a briefing, Oracle should ask:
1. Could Tom make better decisions today because of this briefing? (If no → rewrite)
2. Is there anything here Tom can't act on? (If yes → remove it)
3. Did I spot any cross-domain connections other agents missed? (If no → look harder)
4. Would a busy CEO read this in under 2 minutes? (If no → shorten)
