## 1. EUGENE SCHWARTZ — Breakthrough Advertising

**Core principle:** You don't create desire. You channel existing desire toward your 
product. The market's desire already exists — your job is to connect to it.

**The 5 Levels of Market Awareness (THE most important framework in advertising):**

1. **Most Aware** — Customer knows your product and just needs a deal.
   Copy approach: Direct offer. "20% off today only."
   
2. **Product Aware** — Customer knows your product but hasn't bought yet.
   Copy approach: Prove why yours is best. Testimonials, comparisons, guarantees.
   
3. **Solution Aware** — Customer knows solutions exist but doesn't know your product.
   Copy approach: Show how your mechanism is different/better than alternatives.
   
4. **Problem Aware** — Customer knows they have a problem but doesn't know solutions exist.
   Copy approach: Lead with the problem, agitate it, then introduce your solution.
   
5. **Unaware** — Customer doesn't even know they have a problem.
   Copy approach: Lead with a story, emotion, or provocative statement. Never lead 
   with the product.

**Critical for DBH:** Most supplement customers are Level 3-4. They know they have 
joint pain (problem aware) or know supplements exist (solution aware). The copy must 
match their awareness level EXACTLY. Leading with product features to a Level 4 
person wastes their attention.

**Schwartz on headlines:** "The headline is 80% of the ad. It either stops the scroll 
or loses the sale." The headline's job is not to sell — it's to get the first sentence 
read. The first sentence's job is to get the second sentence read.

**When Meridian should invoke Schwartz:** Every piece of copy. Before writing ANYTHING, 
identify the audience's awareness level first.

## 2. DAVID OGILVY — The Father of Advertising

**Core principle:** "The consumer isn't a moron; she is your wife." Respect the 
intelligence of your audience. Never talk down. Be specific, not vague.

**Operating principles:**
- **Research first:** "Advertising people who ignore research are as dangerous as 
  generals who ignore decodes of enemy signals." Data beats opinion.
- **The brand is a long-term investment:** Every ad should contribute to the long-term 
  personality of the brand. Never sacrifice brand for a short-term sale.
- **Specificity sells:** "The more you tell, the more you sell." Specific claims 
  outperform vague ones. "Reduces joint pain in 14 days" beats "helps with joint pain."
- **Headlines do the heavy lifting:** On average, 5x more people read the headline 
  than the body. If you haven't done your selling in the headline, you've wasted 80% 
  of your money.
- **Long copy sells:** "All the experiments we have ever seen indicate that long 
  copy sells more than short." BUT: every word must earn its place.

**For DBH specifically:**
- Be specific about ingredients: "Contains 100% New Zealand Green Lipped Mussel" not 
  just "natural marine supplement"
- Use real numbers: "10,000+ customers" not "thousands"
- Testimonials with specifics: "I could walk upstairs without pain after 3 weeks" 
  not "great product"

## 3. CLAUDE HOPKINS — Scientific Advertising

**Core principle:** Advertising is salesmanship in print (or on screen). Every dollar 
spent must be trackable. If it can't be measured, don't do it.

**Principles that still dominate DTC:**
- **Test everything:** "Almost any question can be answered, cheaply, quickly and 
  finally, by a test campaign." Never debate when you can test.
- **One thing per ad:** Each ad should make ONE clear proposition. Not three benefits — 
  one. The prospect will remember one thing or nothing.
- **Curiosity is not enough:** An ad that creates curiosity but doesn't direct it 
  toward a specific action is wasted money.
- **Free samples / trials:** The most powerful selling tool is letting people experience 
  the product. Applied to DBH: free trial offers, money-back guarantees, starter packs.
- **Track everything:** "The time has come when advertising in some hands has reached 
  the status of a science." Every campaign must have clear metrics. No vanity spending.

**When Meridian should invoke Hopkins:** Budget allocation decisions, A/B testing 
strategy, and any time someone suggests spending money on "brand awareness" without 
a measurable return mechanism.

## 4. GARY HALBERT — The Prince of Direct Response

**Core principle:** The list is more important than the copy. A mediocre offer to a 
perfect list beats a perfect offer to a mediocre list.

**The A-pile / B-pile concept:** When people sort their mail, some goes to the A-pile 
(personal, must-read) and most goes to the B-pile (junk, ignore). Same applies to 
emails and ads. Your content must feel like A-pile.

**Halbert's Rules:**
- **"What's the starving crowd?"** — The single most important factor in marketing 
  success isn't the product, the copy, or the channel. It's the hunger of the market. 
  Find people who desperately want what you sell.
- **One reader:** Write to ONE specific person, not an audience. DBH: write to Margaret, 
  67, whose knees hurt so much she can't garden anymore. Not "women 55+."
- **Urgency is oxygen:** Every offer needs a reason to act NOW. Not fake urgency — 
  real urgency. Limited stock. Price increase. Seasonal relevance.
- **The "kitchen table" test:** Read your copy out loud. If it sounds like something 
  you'd never say to someone across a kitchen table, rewrite it.

**For DBH email marketing:** The starving crowd is people in PAIN who want relief. 
Lead with their pain, not your features. Write like you're talking to one person 
across a table.

## 5. BYRON SHARP — How Brands Grow

**Core principle:** Most of what marketers believe about brand loyalty and differentiation 
is wrong. Brands grow primarily by reaching MORE people, not by deepening loyalty with 
existing ones.

**The laws that most marketers ignore:**
- **Double Jeopardy Law:** Small brands have fewer buyers AND those buyers buy less 
  frequently. The solution isn't "loyalty programs" — it's reaching more people.
- **All brands share most of their customers:** Your "loyal" customers also buy from 
  competitors. This is normal and unavoidable.
- **Mental availability:** Being thought of in buying situations matters more than being 
  preferred. When someone thinks "joint supplements," does DBH come to mind?
- **Physical availability:** Being easy to buy matters as much as being desired. 
  Distribution, website UX, checkout friction all matter.
- **Distinctive assets > differentiation:** What makes you recognisable (logo, colour, 
  design, tagline) matters more than what makes you "different." People don't choose 
  based on differentiation — they choose based on recognition.

**This challenges conventional marketing thinking.** But it's backed by the largest 
empirical study of brand growth ever conducted. Meridian should hold this in tension 
with direct response principles — Sharp is right about BRAND growth, but Hopkins is 
right about CAMPAIGN measurement.

**For DBH:** Mental availability is the bottleneck. Most NZ supplement buyers don't 
think of DBH when they think "joint health." The primary marketing job is to make DBH 
the first brand that comes to mind.

## 6. SETH GODIN — Permission Marketing

**Core principle:** Interruption marketing is dying. The future belongs to brands that 
earn attention rather than buy it.

**Permission marketing framework:**
1. Offer something valuable in exchange for attention (lead magnet)
2. Deliver value consistently (email nurture)
3. Over time, increase the level of permission (from email to purchase to subscription)
4. Never abuse the permission granted (spam = trust destruction)

**For DBH email marketing:**
- Every email must deliver value OR make a fair exchange (discount for their time)
- The email list is an ASSET, not a channel. Treat subscribers like investors in your brand.
- Unsubscribes from bad emails cost more than the revenue from any single promotion
- The 77.1% open rate from the Gift Psychology formula works BECAUSE it delivers 
  genuine value — it's not a trick, it's permission in action

## SYNTHESIS: THE MERIDIAN DECISION STACK

### Before writing ANY copy:
1. **Schwartz** — What awareness level is this audience? (1-5)
2. **Halbert** — Who specifically am I writing to? (One person, named)
3. **Hopkins** — What is the ONE proposition?
4. **Ogilvy** — Is every claim specific and provable?

### Before launching ANY campaign:
1. **Hopkins** — How will we measure success? What's the test?
2. **Sharp** — Does this increase mental availability (reach) or just deepen loyalty?
3. **Godin** — Are we earning attention or interrupting?
4. **Our proven formulas** — Are we using Trust+Social Proof, Exclusivity+Scarcity, 
   or Gift Psychology? If none, why not?

### Before spending ANY budget:
1. **Halbert** — Is this reaching a "starving crowd" or a lukewarm audience?
2. **Hopkins** — Can we track every dollar to a result?
3. **Sharp** — Are we reaching new people or talking to the same ones?
