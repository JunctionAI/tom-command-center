## 1. DR. LORIMER MOSELEY — Pain Neuroscience

**Core principle:** Pain is a protective output of the brain — not a damage meter. The brain generates pain when it predicts the body needs protection, regardless of whether tissue is still damaged.

**Key insight for Tane's back:**
After 5 years, the tissue injury has largely resolved. What persists is a "danger alarm" locked in the on position. The nervous system learned to protect this area even without ongoing structural threat. This is called central sensitization.

**"Hurt does not equal harm."**
Pain during movement is the nervous system being cautious — not evidence of damage.

**Moseley's three pillars of pain education:**
1. Understand what pain actually is (protection, not damage indicator)
2. Understand your specific pain (what's maintaining it)
3. Engage graded movement exposure (the nervous system learns safety from movement)

**Use with Tane:**
He already has this insight. Reinforce it after flares: "Your back didn't get worse. The alarm system overreacted. The structure is still the same."

**Evidence:** PNE reduces pain intensity, disability, and fear-avoidance behaviors across chronic pain populations (Louw et al., multiple meta-analyses).

## 2. ALAN GORDON — Pain Reprocessing Therapy (PRT)

**Core principle:** Neuroplastic pain — pain generated by the brain as a learned protective response — can be unlearned. The mechanism is teaching the brain that the body is safe.

**PRT method:**
- **Somatic tracking:** Observe pain sensations with curiosity and openness rather than fear. "What does this actually feel like? Where exactly is it? Can I observe it without it meaning something terrible?"
- **Safety reappraisal:** Remind the brain that movement is safe. Do small, curious movements while noticing that the outcome isn't disaster.
- **Corrective experiences:** Each time Tane moves gently and the outcome is OK, he is rewiring the prediction. Not dramatic moments — accumulation of small safe experiences.

**Evidence:** JAMA Psychiatry RCT 2021 (Ashar et al.) — 66% of PRT group pain-free or near pain-free at 1 year vs 10% placebo.

**Use with Tane:**
After a flare settles, encourage a small gentle movement with curiosity: "Notice what it actually feels like. Stay curious. You're not in danger."

Frame: "The Way Out" by Alan Gordon — recommend if he wants to understand his own back on a deeper level.

## 3. DR. BESSEL VAN DER KOLK — Trauma & the Body

**Core principle:** Trauma is stored in the body, not just the mind. The body keeps the score of every unresolved threat. Healing requires bottom-up (body-first) approaches, not only top-down (cognitive) ones.

**Why relevant for Tane:**
Years of hypervigilance and emotional suppression are stored in his back, hips, and jaw as chronic muscle tension. This tension feeds the pain signal. Tane has already named this connection — it's accurate.

**Van der Kolk's recovery framework:**
1. Safety first (nervous system must feel safe before healing can occur)
2. Movement and body-based practices (yoga, somatic work, sports) as primary interventions
3. Social connection (relationships that feel safe are profoundly regulating)
4. Top-down work (therapy, understanding) supports but doesn't replace bottom-up

**For Nova:**
The somatic experiencing therapy Tane is doing is exactly what van der Kolk recommends. Support it between sessions. Don't try to replicate the therapy work.

**Teaching to rotate through:**
"Trauma isn't what happened to you — it's what happened inside you as a result. The healing is in the body."

## 4. DR. STEPHEN PORGES — Polyvagal Theory

**Core principle:** The autonomic nervous system has three hierarchical states: ventral vagal (safe, social), sympathetic (fight/flight), and dorsal vagal (freeze/shutdown). Chronic pain, C-PTSD, and ADHD all tend to produce sympathetic dominance or dorsal collapse.

**The vagus nerve as the key:**
The vagus nerve connects brain to body (heart, lungs, gut, face, voice). Stimulating it activates the parasympathetic — the antidote to chronic threat.

**Direct vagal stimulation techniques (rotate through):**
- Slow exhalation (exhale longer than inhale)
- Humming, chanting, singing (vocal cord vibration activates vagus)
- Cold water on face or cold shower
- Social connection with safe people
- Deep diaphragmatic breathing

**For Tane's 5:30am cortisol awakening:**
Early cortisol spike = sympathetic firing too early. Morning sunlight + gentle movement + slow breathing as first 30 min of day is a direct protocol to complete the stress response before it takes over.

**Teaching to rotate through:**
"The state your body is in determines what your mind can do. When your nervous system is in threat mode, everything feels harder. Regulating the body first isn't self-indulgent — it's the precondition for everything else."

## 5. DR. RUSSELL BARKLEY — ADHD & Executive Function

**Core principle:** ADHD is fundamentally a disorder of self-regulation over time — not attention per se. The inability to hold future consequences in mind when making present decisions is the core deficit.

**Key insight for Tane:**
ADHD makes consistent habit formation genuinely harder — not a character flaw. The habits that would heal his back (daily mobilization, consistent sleep, gym attendance, regular meals) require sustained initiation and follow-through across time. This is exactly what ADHD makes hardest.

**What helps:**
- External cues and accountability (Nova's role)
- Breaking tasks into single steps (never give 3 things — give 1)
- Making habits concrete and specific ("do 10 minutes of cat-cow after your morning coffee" vs "do morning mobilization")
- Reducing friction (mat out the night before, no decision-making required in the morning)
- Immediate feedback loops (tracking, checking in) matter more than future rewards

**Vyvanse + ADHD:**
Lisdexamfetamine (Vyvanse) improves dopamine availability in prefrontal cortex — directly addressing the initiation and regulation deficits. Monitor: does Tane report more consistency on Vyvanse days?

**Teaching to rotate through:**
"ADHD isn't a willpower problem. It's a timing problem — the future doesn't feel real enough yet to compete with the present. The fix isn't trying harder. It's building structures that bring the future into now."

## 6. DR. MATTHEW WALKER — Sleep Science

**Core principle:** Sleep is the master regulation system. Every aspect of physical and mental health — pain, mood, memory, motivation, immune function, cortisol — is directly downstream of sleep quality.

**Why Tane's sleep is critical:**
Unrestorative sleep amplifies pain sensitivity (Walker: "sleep deprivation is a pain amplifier"). Poor sleep + chronic pain is a bidirectional loop — each makes the other worse.

**Sleep apnoea (Tane's suspected issue):**
If sleep apnoea is confirmed and treated, this could be the single biggest change in his symptom picture. Oxygen desaturation from apnoea disrupts every sleep stage and drives cortisol dysregulation.

**5:30am activation:**
Classic cortisol awakening response dysregulation. The healthy pattern is gradual cortisol rise. Tane's pattern suggests the HPA is firing too aggressively early. Treatment: regulating cortisol through the day (exercise, sleep consistency, reducing stress load).

**Sleep hygiene for Tane (without triggering the "sleep restriction warning"):**
- Consistent wake time (even if didn't sleep well)
- Morning light within 30 min of waking — powerful circadian anchor
- Avoid caffeine after midday
- Dark, cool room (≤18°C ideal)
- No screens 30-60 min before sleep (if possible — this is ADHD-hard)
- Magnesium glycinate at night (already taking — good)
- Cannabis: monitor if use correlates with worse REM/quality

**Teaching to rotate through:**
"Every hour of poor sleep lowers your pain threshold the next day. Your back and your sleep are in a two-way conversation. Fixing sleep is back pain treatment."

## 7. DR. JAMES GORDON — Mind-Body Medicine

**Core principle:** Chronic illness exists at the intersection of physical, emotional, and social factors. Healing requires addressing all three — not just the physical symptom.

**For Tane:**
His back pain, ADHD, C-PTSD, poor sleep, and low energy are not separate problems. They're one interconnected system. Treating each in isolation (more physio, more medication, more supplements) without addressing the whole system explains why things haven't resolved.

**Relevant tools:**
- Journaling (emotional processing as pain management)
- Creative expression (music — Tane is already a touring musician. This is medicine.)
- Imagery and visualisation for pain (evidence-backed for chronic pain)
- Social reconnection (safe relationships are regulatory)

**For Nova:**
Music is Tane's natural healing outlet. When he plays, performs, or creates — this is polyvagal regulation, dopamine, social connection, and creative expression in one. Acknowledge this when it comes up.
