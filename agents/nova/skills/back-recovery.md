## THE CORE MODEL: TANE'S BACK IS A NERVOUS SYSTEM PROBLEM

After 5 years with L5/S1 disc injury and no sustained recovery despite physio, chiro, and osteo — the most likely explanation is **central sensitization / neuroplastic pain**.

**What this means:**
The original injury likely partially healed years ago. What persists is a nervous system that has learned to produce pain as a protective response — even when the structural threat is reduced. The brain has "memorized" the danger signal. Every time Tane braces, guards, or avoids movement, he reinforces the threat message.

Manual therapy works temporarily because it briefly signals safety to the nervous system — not because it fixes structure. This is why the relief lasts only ~1 week.

**What this doesn't mean:**
- It doesn't mean the pain is fake. Central sensitization pain is REAL pain — it's just generated by the nervous system, not by tissue damage.
- It doesn't mean structure doesn't matter at all. The L5/S1 disc is a genuine issue. But the ongoing pain is disproportionate to any remaining structural problem.

**Key insight to use with Tane (once only — don't repeat):**
"Your nervous system learned this pain pattern during a period of high stress, real injury, and repeated re-injury. The back healed enough, but the protective response stayed locked on. The treatment path is teaching the nervous system you're safe — not more aggressive physio."

## PAIN FRAMEWORKS

### Pain Reprocessing Therapy (PRT) — Alan Gordon
- Core claim: Neuroplastic pain (pain generated by the nervous system, not ongoing tissue damage) can be eliminated by changing how the brain interprets body signals.
- Method: Somatic tracking (observe pain sensations with curiosity rather than fear), safety reappraisal, corrective experiences through movement
- Evidence: RCT 2021 (Ashar et al.) — 66% PRT group pain-free at 1 year vs 10% placebo. This is significant.
- How to apply with Tane: Frame movement as safe. Encourage noticing pain without catastrophizing. "That sensation is your nervous system being overprotective — it doesn't mean damage."
- Resource if Tane wants to go deeper: "The Way Out" by Alan Gordon

### Pain Neuroscience Education (PNE) — Lorimer Moseley
- Understanding pain reduces it. When people understand that pain is a protection response, not a damage meter, their pain decreases.
- Tane already has this insight ("closely linked with trauma and nervous system patterns"). Don't re-explain. Build on it.
- Key phrase: "Hurt does not equal harm."

### McGill Big 3 — Dr. Stuart McGill
- Prescribed by Tane's 2021 sports specialist. Evidence-based for L5/S1 discogenic pain.
- Three exercises targeting spinal stability without loading:
  1. **Modified curl-up:** Hands under lumbar curve, one leg bent one straight, small head lift only. NOT a sit-up. 3 sets of (8, 6, 4) decreasing reps with 8-sec holds.
  2. **Side plank:** Modified (knee down) or full. Hold 8-10 sec, rest, repeat. Build duration over weeks.
  3. **Bird-dog:** On all fours, extend opposite arm + leg. Hold 8-10 sec. Keep neutral spine. 3 sets each side.
- ⚠️ CRITICAL: Confirm with physio before prescribing. If physio has modified these or replaced them, follow physio's program.

## MOVEMENT IS MEDICINE (not injury management)

**The wrong frame:** "My back hurts, so I should rest and avoid movement until it heals."
**The right frame:** "Graded movement teaches my nervous system that movement is safe."

Daily walking is one of the most evidence-supported interventions for chronic back pain. Not because it "strengthens" the back — because it signals safety.

**Graded exposure principle:**
- Start with what's comfortable. Do it consistently. Note that pain doesn't increase with it.
- Gradually expand the range of comfortable movement.
- Do NOT push through 8+/10 pain. But 4-5/10 discomfort during movement is not damage — it's the nervous system recalibrating.

**Body positions for back pain relief (acute or flare):**
- 90/90 position: Lie on back, hips and knees at 90°, feet on a chair or sofa. Decompresses L5/S1.
- Cat-cow: Slow, rhythmic, 10 reps. Mobilizes without loading.
- Child's pose (if comfortable): Gentle spinal flexion decompression.
- Walking: Even 5-10 min during a flare is better than lying still.

## FLARE-UP MANAGEMENT

**During a flare (7+/10):**
- Validate first: "That's brutal. Let's manage this."
- Heat 20 min (NOT ice for chronic back pain — heat signals safety to nervous system)
- Move gently: do NOT lie still >30 min. Even cat-cow or walking is better than immobility.
- 90/90 position for relief
- Breathing: slow exhale. Activating parasympathetic reduces muscle guarding.
- Avoid bending, twisting, or loading
- If >48 hours → contact physio
- ⚠️ Do NOT say "rest completely" — immobility reinforces central sensitization

**After a flare:**
"Flares are the nervous system temporarily going into high alert. The structure didn't get worse. You're still on the same trajectory. The next step after the flare settles is the same as it was before."
