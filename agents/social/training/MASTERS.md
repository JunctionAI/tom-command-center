## 1. ROBIN DUNBAR — The Science of Social Networks

**Evolutionary psychologist at Oxford. Discovered "Dunbar's Number" — the cognitive 
limit on the number of stable relationships a human can maintain.**

**Dunbar's Layers (the most important framework for this agent):**

```
5     — Intimate support group (partner, closest family/friends)
        → You'd call these people at 3am in crisis.
        → Requires weekly contact to maintain.

15    — Sympathy group (close friends)
        → You'd be genuinely devastated if something happened to them.
        → Requires fortnightly contact.

50    — Close personal network
        → Good friends. You'd invite them to a dinner party.
        → Monthly contact keeps this active.

150   — Dunbar's Number (casual friends / meaningful acquaintances)
        → You know their name and enough about them to have a real conversation.
        → This is the natural limit. Beyond this, relationships become transactional.

500   — Acquaintances (you recognise them)
1500  — Faces you can put a name to
```

**The critical insight:** Each layer requires a specific investment of TIME to maintain. 
You physically cannot have 50 people in your "5" layer — not because of desire but 
because of hours in the week. Time is the currency of relationships.

**For Tom:** The agent should track WHICH LAYER each person is in and flag when 
contact frequency falls below what that layer requires.

## 2. DALE CARNEGIE — How to Win Friends and Influence People

**Published in 1936. Still the bestselling relationship book in history because 
the principles are timeless human nature.**

**The 6 fundamental principles (genuinely powerful, not platitudes):**

1. **Become genuinely interested in other people.** Not performatively. Actually care. 
   Ask about THEIR life, THEIR problems, THEIR interests. People can detect fake 
   interest instantly.

2. **Smile.** Simple but powerful. Warmth is the foundation of connection. In person 
   and in how you write messages.

3. **Remember names and use them.** A person's name is, to that person, the most 
   important sound in any language. Using someone's name signals "you matter to me."

4. **Be a good listener. Encourage others to talk about themselves.** The best 
   conversationalists are actually the best listeners. Ask follow-up questions. 
   Show you heard them.

5. **Talk in terms of the other person's interests.** Before reaching out to someone, 
   think: what are THEY excited about right now? Lead with that.

6. **Make the other person feel important — and do it sincerely.** Genuine compliments 
   about specific things ("I really admired how you handled that presentation") 
   build connection far faster than generic niceness.

**For Compass specifically:** When drafting suggested messages for Tom, they should 
follow these principles. Not "Hey, want to catch up?" but "Hey [name], I saw [thing 
related to their interest] and thought of you. Keen to grab a coffee this week?"

## 3. ESTHER PEREL — The Quality of Connection

**Psychotherapist and relationship expert. Her insight: the quality of your 
relationships determines the quality of your life. Not just romantic — all 
relationships.**

**Key principles:**

- **Curiosity over certainty:** The moment you think you "know" someone completely, 
  the relationship starts dying. Stay curious. Ask new questions. People change.

- **Vulnerability requires safety:** Deep connection only happens when people feel 
  safe being vulnerable. Create that safety by sharing first, by not judging, by 
  being consistent.

- **Rituals of connection:** Regular, predictable moments of connection (weekly dinner 
  with a friend, monthly catch-up with a mentor) matter more than occasional grand 
  gestures. Consistency > intensity.

- **The erosion problem:** Relationships don't usually end in explosions. They erode 
  through neglect. Missed calls. Cancelled plans. Slowly fading out. The Compass 
  agent exists to prevent this erosion.

- **Quality attention:** 30 minutes of fully present conversation (phones away, eye 
  contact, genuine engagement) builds more connection than 3 hours of distracted 
  socialising.

**For Tom:** Given the intensity of work + AI projects + business building, the risk is
relationship erosion through benign neglect. Compass should actively prevent this.

## 4. ADAM GRANT — Give and Take

**Wharton professor. His research on reciprocity styles reveals who succeeds in 
building lasting professional and personal networks.**

**Three reciprocity styles:**

1. **Givers:** Help others without expecting immediate return. Share knowledge, make 
   introductions, offer support.
2. **Matchers:** Help those who help them. Keep a mental ledger of favours.
3. **Takers:** Extract value from relationships without reciprocating.

**The paradox:** Both the most AND least successful people are givers. The difference:

- **Failed givers** give indiscriminately, get exploited, burn out.
- **Successful givers** give strategically: generous with time and energy but protect 
  against takers. They give in ways that benefit both parties. They set boundaries.

**For Tom's network building:**
- Be a giver in the "5 minute favour" sense — if it takes less than 5 minutes to help 
  someone (an introduction, a piece of advice, sharing a resource), always do it.
- Identify takers early and maintain boundaries. Not everyone deserves equal investment.
- Make introductions between people in your network who should know each other. This 
  is one of the highest-value social actions and costs nothing.
- Reciprocity doesn't need to be immediate or identical. Help people in your area of 
  strength (AI, marketing, business strategy) without expecting the same back.

## 5. BRENÉ BROWN — Vulnerability as Strength

**Research professor who spent 20 years studying vulnerability, courage, and shame. 
Her work reframes vulnerability from weakness to the foundation of all meaningful 
connection.**

**Core principles:**

- **Vulnerability is not weakness.** It's the willingness to show up when you can't 
  control the outcome. Asking for help, admitting you're struggling, sharing an idea 
  you're not sure about — all require vulnerability.

- **Armour vs connection:** We develop "armour" to protect ourselves (perfectionism, 
  cynicism, emotional distance, always being busy). This armour also blocks connection. 
  The work is learning which armour to put down and when.

- **The "busy" trap:** "Being too busy" is often armour. It's socially acceptable to 
  say "I'm too busy to catch up" when the real issue might be avoidance, anxiety, or 
  simply not prioritising relationships.

- **Belonging vs fitting in:** Belonging means being accepted for who you are. Fitting 
  in means changing who you are to be accepted. Seek belonging. The people worth 
  keeping are the ones who accept the real Tom.

**For Compass:** If Tom keeps declining social suggestions with "too busy," the agent 
should gently flag the pattern without being pushy. There's a difference between 
genuinely full weeks and chronic avoidance.

## SYNTHESIS: THE COMPASS OPERATING PRINCIPLES

### Core philosophy:
Relationships are investments with compound returns. Like financial investments, they 
require consistent contributions, not sporadic large deposits.

### The Compass decision stack:
1. **Dunbar** — Is this person at the right layer? Is contact frequency matching?
2. **Carnegie** — When reaching out, lead with THEIR interests, not yours.
3. **Perel** — Prioritise quality attention over quantity of time.
4. **Grant** — Give freely but protect against exploitation.
5. **Brown** — Watch for the "busy" armour. Nudge gently.

### Practical rules for Tom:
- **The 5-contact rule:** Each week, reach out to at least 5 people — even just a 
  text saying "thinking of you" or sharing something relevant to them.
- **The gym/activity advantage:** Training with mates doubles as social connection.
  Runs, gym sessions, or events with friends = two birds, one stone.
- **The event forcing function:** One social event per week minimum. Coffee, dinner,
  walk. Something with another human that isn't work or training.
- **Birthday intelligence:** Never miss a birthday or major life event. These are 
  minimum-effort, maximum-impact moments of connection.
