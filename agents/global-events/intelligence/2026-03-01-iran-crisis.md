## CRITICAL ANALYSIS

### Through the Masters' Lenses

**FRIEDMAN (Geographic Imperatives)**:
Iran controls the Strait of Hormuz -- 20% of global oil supply (20M barrels/day). This is Iran's ultimate geographic leverage. Closing it is the economic nuclear option. Iran's mountainous terrain makes ground invasion extremely costly (unlike Iraq's flat desert). US/Israel can bomb but cannot easily occupy.

**DALIO (Macro Cycle)**:
This is a potential accelerant of the debt cycle crisis. Oil spike to $80-100+/barrel adds 0.6-0.7 percentage points to global inflation. Markets will react violently Monday. Gold already up 22% in 2026, expect further surge. If Hormuz stays closed, global recession risk increases dramatically. This is a stagflationary shock.

**ZEIHAN (Demographics/Trade)**:
Iran's demographic profile was already troubled. Regime change creates a power vacuum in a country of 88M people. If Iranian oil comes offline (4% of global supply) AND Hormuz restricts Saudi/Iraqi/Kuwaiti oil transit, we're looking at the biggest supply shock since 1973. NZ is relatively insulated on energy but dependent on Asian trade which will be disrupted.

**TALEB (Black Swan/Antifragility)**:
This IS the black swan for 2026. The tail risk scenario: Hormuz closure + proxy escalation + cyberattacks = cascading system failure. Key question: are we antifragile to this? NZ's isolation is an advantage. DBH's supply chain exposure to Asian shipping needs monitoring.

**BREMMER (Political Risk)**:
US-Iran is now in open war, not just strikes. Iran is on "death ground" (Sun Tzu) -- existential threat creates maximum unpredictability. Russia and China issued pro-Iran statements. This deepens the US/West vs Russia/China bifurcation. NZ is firmly in the US camp but economically tied to China -- the tension Bremmer predicted is materialising.

**KENNAN (Strategic Containment)**:
The containment strategy has been abandoned for direct action. This is the opposite of Kennan's approach. The risk: decapitation (killing Khamenei) creates chaos, not necessarily the desired outcome. Power vacuums in the Middle East (Iraq 2003, Libya 2011) have historically produced worse outcomes than the regimes they replaced.
