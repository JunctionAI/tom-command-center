## 1. ROBERT RODRIGUEZ — Rebel Without a Crew (Constraint as Creativity)

**Made "El Mariachi" for $7,000 and launched a Hollywood career. The godfather of 
doing more with less. His philosophy is THE operating system for AI filmmaking.**

**Core principle:** Constraints don't limit creativity — they fuel it. The less you 
have, the more creative you must be. This isn't a consolation prize. This is an 
actual advantage over people with unlimited budgets.

**The Rodriguez Method:**
1. **Inventory your resources FIRST:** Don't write a script then figure out how to 
   shoot it. Look at what you HAVE (locations, props, actors, tools) and write TO 
   those resources.
2. **Do everything yourself:** Director, editor, cinematographer, sound. Not because 
   you can't hire people, but because understanding every role makes you a better 
   filmmaker. Applied to AI: learn every tool yourself before delegating.
3. **Speed is a feature:** Shoot fast. Edit fast. Release fast. The faster you 
   iterate, the more you learn. Perfectionism on a low budget is death.
4. **The "10 minute film school":** Every technical problem has a creative solution. 
   Can't afford a crane shot? Tell the story differently. AI model can't do action? 
   Imply the action through editing, sound, and reaction shots.

**For Tom's AI filmmaking:**
- The 100 Men vs Gorilla project IS Rodriguez thinking — make an ambitious film with 
  AI tools instead of a Hollywood budget
- When a model can't do something, don't wait for it to improve — find a creative 
  workaround using editing, compositing, or clever prompting
- Speed of iteration matters more than perfection of any single shot

## 2. WALTER MURCH — In the Blink of an Eye (The Art of Editing)

**Legendary film editor (Apocalypse Now, The English Patient, The Godfather trilogy). 
Wrote the definitive text on why editing works and how to think about assembling footage.**

**The Rule of Six (what makes a good cut, in priority order):**
1. **Emotion** (51%) — Does the cut serve the emotion of the moment?
2. **Story** (23%) — Does the cut advance the narrative?
3. **Rhythm** (10%) — Does the cut feel right temporally?
4. **Eye trace** (7%) — Does the viewer's eye land naturally in the new frame?
5. **2D screen space** (5%) — Does the composition of the new shot work?
6. **3D spatial continuity** (4%) — Does it maintain spatial logic?

**The critical insight:** Most beginners obsess about #5 and #6 (making it look 
"right" technically) while ignoring #1 and #2 (emotion and story). A technically 
"wrong" cut that serves emotion WORKS. A technically "perfect" cut that kills 
emotion FAILS.

**For AI-generated content:**
- AI clips will have inconsistencies (lighting, character appearance, physics)
- If the EMOTION and STORY are right, audiences forgive technical imperfection
- Edit for feeling first, continuity second
- Sound design and music can carry emotional coherence even when visuals have 
  AI artifacts

**Murch on rhythm:** "The ideal cut is like blinking. You cut at the moment the 
audience's brain naturally wants to reset. If you watch someone speaking and their 
thought shifts, that's where you blink — and that's where you should cut."

## 3. DAN HARMON — The Story Circle (Simplified Hero's Journey)

**Creator of Community and Rick and Morty. Distilled Joseph Campbell's hero's journey 
into an 8-step story circle that works for everything from 30-second ads to feature films.**

**The Story Circle:**
```
    1. A character is in a zone of comfort (YOU)
    2. But they WANT something
    3. They enter an unfamiliar situation (GO)
    4. They adapt to it (SEARCH)
    5. They get what they wanted (FIND)
    6. But pay a heavy price for it (TAKE)
    7. Then return to their familiar situation (RETURN)
    8. Having changed (CHANGE)
```

**Applied to a 30-second Pure Pets ad:**
1. Your dog is part of your family (comfort)
2. But you see them struggling to walk (want — wanting them healthy)
3. You try everything — vet bills, other supplements (unfamiliar situation)
4. You discover Pure Pets NZ Green Lipped Mussel (adapt)
5. Your dog starts running again (get what you wanted)
6. You wish you'd found it sooner (price — emotional beat)
7. Back to normal life, but better (return)
8. Your dog is a puppy again. You're a better pet parent. (change)

**For AI video content:**
- EVERY piece of content should follow some version of this circle
- Even a 15-second clip needs steps 1-2-5-8 at minimum (comfort → want → find → change)
- The "change" at the end is what makes content memorable and shareable

## 4. ED CATMULL — Creativity, Inc. (Managing the Creative Process)

**Co-founder of Pixar. Built the most consistently excellent creative studio in 
history. His insights are about managing the PROCESS of making great things.**

**Core principles:**

- **All films suck at first.** Every Pixar movie started as a terrible idea. The 
  process of making it great is what matters. Applied to AI filmmaking: the first 
  generation will look rough. The iteration process is where quality lives.

- **The Braintrust:** Regularly show your work to trusted people who will be honest. 
  Not for approval — for candid feedback. The key rule: the Braintrust can identify 
  problems but cannot prescribe solutions. Only the creator decides how to fix it.

- **Protect the new:** New ideas are fragile and ugly. They need protection from 
  premature judgment. Give an idea time to develop before deciding it's bad.

- **Failure is not a necessary evil. It's a necessary consequence of doing something 
  new.** If you're not failing occasionally, you're not pushing hard enough.

- **Don't confuse the process with the goal.** The goal is a great film. The process 
  is a tool. If the process isn't producing good results, change the process.

**For Tom's creative workflow:**
- First drafts of AI-generated content WILL be rough. That's normal.
- Build a feedback loop: generate → review → refine prompt → regenerate → edit
- Don't show early AI output to people who'll judge it prematurely. Protect the new.
- Track iteration counts: how many generations to get a usable shot? This metric 
  improves over time.

## 5. CASEY NEISTAT — Authentic Storytelling at Speed

**YouTube pioneer who proved that authentic, high-energy content beats polished 
corporate production. His approach to daily vlogging created a blueprint for 
content creation at scale.**

**Core principles:**

- **Story > production quality.** A compelling story shot on a phone beats a boring 
  story shot on a RED camera. Every time.
- **Quantity leads to quality.** You get better by MAKING THINGS, not by planning them. 
  Neistat's daily vlog forced him to tell a story every single day. His best work came 
  from that volume.
- **Energy is contagious.** High energy, fast pacing, genuine enthusiasm are more 
  important than technique. Audiences feel energy through the screen.
- **Start before you're ready.** The biggest creative killer is waiting for perfect 
  conditions. Perfect conditions never come. Start with what you have.
- **The 3-act structure for short-form:** Every piece of content: Hook (0-3 sec) → 
  Development (the middle) → Payoff (the end). If the hook doesn't work, nothing 
  else matters.

**For AI video content production:**
- Don't wait for the "perfect" AI model. Start generating with what's available.
- Build a volume pipeline: generate many, select the best, iterate on winners
- The hook is everything in social content. First 3 seconds decide if anyone watches.
- Test 10 hooks to find 1 that works. This is a numbers game.

## 6. CHRISTOPHER NOLAN — Practical Innovation

**Director who pushes the boundaries of filmmaking while respecting the craft. Known 
for innovative storytelling (Memento, Inception, Tenet) and blending practical effects 
with technology.**

**Principles relevant to AI filmmaking:**

- **Serve the story, not the technology.** Never use a technique because it's available. 
  Use it because the story demands it. AI generation should be invisible to the viewer — 
  they should see a story, not "AI-generated content."
- **Non-linear storytelling:** Playing with time, perspective, and structure can 
  compensate for technical limitations. If you can't show something directly, show 
  it from another angle, another time, another perspective.
- **The power of IMAX thinking:** Even at the earliest stage, think about how the 
  content will be experienced. Social media is "small screen IMAX" — every frame must 
  read clearly at phone size.
- **Practical + digital hybrid:** The best results often come from combining AI-generated 
  elements with real footage, real audio, real human performances. Pure AI is limited. 
  Hybrid is powerful.

## SYNTHESIS: THE LENS CREATIVE STACK

### For every creative project:
1. **Rodriguez** — What resources do I have? Write to the tools, not against them.
2. **Harmon** — What's the story circle? Even for a 15-second clip.
3. **Murch** — Edit for emotion first, technical perfection second.
4. **Catmull** — Protect early ideas. Iterate relentlessly. First drafts always suck.
5. **Neistat** — Start now. Volume beats perfection. Hook is everything.
6. **Nolan** — Serve the story, not the tech. Think hybrid.

### For AI model evaluation:
When a new model drops, evaluate against:
- Can it serve the STORY we need to tell? (Nolan)
- Can we iterate quickly with it? (Neistat/Rodriguez)
- Does the output carry EMOTION even if imperfect? (Murch)
- What creative WORKAROUNDS exist for its limitations? (Rodriguez)

### The 100 Men vs Gorilla urgency:
- The project is paused for a technical reason (no action-capable model)
- Rodriguez would say: "Is there a creative workaround using editing and implication?"
- Murch would say: "Can sound design and reaction shots carry the fight scenes?"
- Consider: partial AI + practical/creative editing solutions vs. waiting indefinitely
