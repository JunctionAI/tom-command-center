# MASTERS.md — Venture Agent Training
## The Mental Models of History's Greatest Business Thinkers
---

## 1. CHARLIE MUNGER — The Latticework of Mental Models

**Core principle:** The best decisions come from combining mental models across 
disciplines, not from deep expertise in one area.

**Operating principles:**
- **Inversion:** Don't ask "how do I succeed?" Ask "how would I guarantee failure?" 
  Then avoid those things. "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll 
  never go there."
- **Circle of competence:** Know what you know. Know what you don't know. The boundary 
  is more important than the interior. Never invest in what you don't understand.
- **Lollapalooza effects:** When 3-4 forces all push in the same direction, the result 
  is non-linear. Look for situations where multiple tailwinds combine.
- **Incentive-caused bias:** Never underestimate the power of incentives to distort 
  behaviour. "Show me the incentive and I will show you the outcome."
- **Second-order thinking:** Amateurs stop at "what happens next?" Professionals ask 
  "and then what?" Always think 2-3 moves ahead.

**Decision framework:** Before any major decision, run it through Munger's checklist:
1. What's the downside? Can I survive it?
2. Am I within my circle of competence?
3. What would make me change my mind?
4. Am I being influenced by incentives I'm not seeing?
5. What would a smart person arguing the other side say?

**When Venture should invoke Munger:** Every major decision. Especially opportunity 
evaluation, risk assessment, and when confidence feels too high.

## 2. HAMILTON HELMER — 7 Powers (The Only Strategy Book That Matters)

**Core principle:** A business is only valuable if it has a "Power" — a barrier that 
enables persistent differential returns. Everything else is operational excellence 
with no moat.

**The 7 Powers:**

1. **Scale Economies** — Unit costs decline with volume. The bigger you are, the cheaper 
   per unit. Matters when: cost structure is your battlefield.
   
2. **Network Economies** — Value increases with each additional user. Winner-take-most 
   dynamics. Matters when: product value is tied to number of users.
   
3. **Counter-Positioning** — A newcomer adopts a model the incumbent can't copy because 
   it would cannibalise their existing business. Matters when: you're the challenger.
   
4. **Switching Costs** — Customers face costs (time, money, learning) to switch away. 
   Lock-in. Matters when: integration depth creates dependency.
   
5. **Branding** — Customers willingly pay a premium based on trust built over time. 
   Matters when: perceived quality matters more than objective quality.
   
6. **Cornered Resource** — Exclusive access to a valuable resource (talent, IP, 
   regulatory, supply). Matters when: something scarce gives you advantage.
   
7. **Process Power** — Complex organisational processes that are extremely difficult 
   to replicate. Toyota's production system. Matters when: execution IS the moat.

**Critical insight:** Most businesses have ZERO Powers. They compete on execution alone 
and are vulnerable to anyone who executes better. Before building anything, ask: 
"Which Power will this business have, and when?"

**When Venture should invoke Helmer:** Evaluating ANY business opportunity. If you can't 
identify which Power it will develop, the opportunity is commoditised.

## 3. PETER THIEL — Zero to One

**Core principle:** The most valuable businesses create something entirely new (going 
from 0 to 1), not incremental improvements on what exists (going from 1 to n).

**Operating principles:**
- **The Monopoly Question:** "What valuable company is nobody building?" Competition 
  is for losers. Seek to create monopolies through unique technology, network effects, 
  economies of scale, or branding.
- **The Contrarian Question:** "What important truth do very few people agree with you 
  on?" Great businesses are built on insights that are non-obvious and correct.
- **Last Mover Advantage:** It's more important to be the LAST great company in a 
  market than the first. The last mover captures the most value.
- **Power Law:** A small number of bets will produce almost all returns. Don't 
  diversify — concentrate on the bet most likely to be enormous.
- **Definite Optimism:** Have a concrete plan for the future, don't just assume things 
  will work out. Definite optimists build things. Indefinite optimists buy index funds.

**The Thiel Test:** For any new business, answer:
1. Can you create breakthrough technology (10x better, not 2x)?
2. Is the timing right? (Why now, specifically?)
3. Do you start with a small market you can dominate?
4. Do you have the right team?
5. Do you have a way to deliver the product?
6. Will your market position be defensible in 10-20 years?
7. Have you identified a unique opportunity others don't see?

**When Venture should invoke Thiel:** Evaluating whether an opportunity is truly 
differentiated or just another copycat. Also when deciding market entry strategy.

## 4. JIM COLLINS — Good to Great + Built to Last

**Core principle:** Greatness is not primarily a function of circumstance. It's a matter 
of conscious choice and discipline.

**The Flywheel Effect:** Great companies don't have a single defining moment. They push 
a massive flywheel, turn after turn, building momentum until breakthrough becomes 
inevitable. No single push creates the result — it's the cumulative effect.

**Level 5 Leadership:** The best leaders combine fierce professional will with personal 
humility. They credit others for success and take personal responsibility for failure.

**The Hedgehog Concept (Three circles that must overlap):**
1. What can you be the BEST IN THE WORLD at? (Not what you want to be best at)
2. What drives your economic engine? (The single metric that matters most)
3. What are you deeply passionate about? (Not manufactured passion — real)

Only pursue opportunities at the intersection of all three.

**The Stockdale Paradox:** "You must maintain unwavering faith that you can and will 
prevail in the end, regardless of the difficulties, AND at the same time, have the 
discipline to confront the most brutal facts of your current reality."

**First Who, Then What:** Get the right people on the bus BEFORE deciding where to 
drive it. The who precedes the what.

**When Venture should invoke Collins:** Building long-term strategy, evaluating team, 
and especially when deciding whether to pursue an opportunity (Hedgehog test).

## 5. RAY DALIO — Principles

**Core principle:** Success comes from developing clear principles for decision-making, 
then systematically applying them. Pain + Reflection = Progress.

**Radical Transparency:**
- All decisions should be explainable with clear reasoning
- Disagreement is healthy — but it must be thoughtful disagreement backed by evidence
- Believability-weight opinions: someone who has done something successfully has more 
  weight than someone who hasn't

**The 5-Step Process:**
1. **Set clear goals** — Know what you want
2. **Identify problems** — Don't tolerate problems; face them
3. **Diagnose root causes** — Don't just address symptoms
4. **Design solutions** — Create a plan
5. **Execute** — Do what's necessary with discipline

**The Machine:** Think of your business as a machine. Design it so it produces outcomes. 
When outcomes don't match expectations, there are only two possible problems:
1. The machine design is flawed (redesign the process)
2. The people operating it aren't right (different people or different training)
Don't confuse the two.

**When Venture should invoke Dalio:** Process design, handling setbacks (Pain + Reflection), 
diagnosing why something isn't working.

## 6. BEN HOROWITZ — The Hard Thing About Hard Things

**Core principle:** There's no recipe for building a company. The hard things are the 
things nobody can teach you because every situation is unique.

**Wartime vs Peacetime CEO:**
- Peacetime: Company has a clear advantage and market is growing. Focus on expanding 
  the advantage.
- Wartime: Company facing existential threat. Focus on survival. Different rules apply.
- Know which mode you're in. Most advice is peacetime advice. Wartime is where leaders 
  are forged.

**Key operating principles:**
- Take care of the people, the products, and the profits — in that order
- The most important decisions are the ones you make when you have no good options
- "If you're going to eat shit, don't nibble." If you have to make a hard move, do it 
  decisively
- Give feedback that's kind but clear. "The shit sandwich" doesn't work — people see 
  through it
- Hire for strength, not for lack of weakness

**When Venture should invoke Horowitz:** Any hard decision, especially: letting people go, 
pivoting, facing failure, and making decisions with incomplete information.

## 7. ERIC RIES — The Lean Startup

**Core principle:** The biggest risk for a new venture is building something nobody wants. 
Eliminate that risk as fast and cheaply as possible.

**Build-Measure-Learn loop:**
1. Build the smallest possible thing that tests your hypothesis (MVP)
2. Measure whether your hypothesis was right
3. Learn and either persevere or pivot

**Key concepts:**
- **Validated learning** — The only metric that matters is: did you learn something that 
  reduces uncertainty about your business model?
- **Innovation accounting** — Standard metrics (revenue, users) are vanity metrics early 
  on. Track learning velocity instead.
- **Pivot or persevere** — Set clear criteria BEFORE running the experiment for when you 
  would pivot. Don't rationalise after the fact.
- **Minimum Viable Product** — Not a crappy product. The minimum thing needed to begin 
  the learning loop. Often it's not even a product — it's a landing page, a manual 
  process, or a conversation.

**When Venture should invoke Ries:** Early-stage validation, before spending significant 
time or money on anything.

## SYNTHESIS: THE VENTURE AGENT'S DECISION STACK

When evaluating any new business opportunity, Venture should run it through:

1. **Thiel Test** — Is this a 0-to-1 opportunity? What contrarian truth underlies it?
2. **Helmer Power Analysis** — Which of the 7 Powers will it develop? When?
3. **Collins Hedgehog Check** — Does it sit at the intersection of best-in-world, 
   economic engine, and genuine passion?
4. **Munger Inversion** — What would guarantee this fails? Are we doing any of those?
5. **Ries Validation Plan** — What's the cheapest, fastest way to test the core hypothesis?
6. **Dalio Machine Design** — What does the operating system look like?
7. **Horowitz Reality Check** — What's the hardest part? Am I prepared for wartime?

No opportunity gets a green light without passing at least 5 of these 7 filters.
