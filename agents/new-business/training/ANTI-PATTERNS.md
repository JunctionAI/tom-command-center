## What the Best Avoid (and What Kills Most Ventures)

## THE 10 DEADLIEST ANTI-PATTERNS

### 1. Building Before Validating
**What it looks like:** Spending weeks/months building a product before talking to a 
single customer. "If we build it, they will come."
**Why it kills:** 90% of startups fail because they build something nobody wants.
**The masters' view:** Ries — "The biggest risk is market risk, not technical risk."
**Counter-principle:** Validate BEFORE building. The Lean Validation Ladder exists 
specifically to prevent this.

### 2. The Passion Trap
**What it looks like:** "I love this idea so much, I don't need to validate it." 
Falling in love with the solution instead of the problem.
**Why it kills:** Passion without market signal is expensive hobby, not a business.
**The masters' view:** Collins — Passion is only 1/3 of the Hedgehog. Without the 
other two (best-in-world + economic engine), it's delusion.
**Counter-principle:** Test EVERY idea against all three circles. No exceptions.

### 3. Premature Scaling
**What it looks like:** Hiring people, spending on marketing, building infrastructure 
before achieving product-market fit.
**Why it kills:** Scaling a broken model just makes it break faster and more expensively.
**The masters' view:** Ries — The only metric that matters early is: are you learning?
**Counter-principle:** Do things that don't scale first (Concierge MVP). Scale comes 
after validated learning, not before.

### 4. The Competition Obsession
**What it looks like:** Constant monitoring of competitors. Reacting to their moves. 
Copying their features.
**Why it kills:** You stop building something unique and start building a slightly 
different version of what already exists.
**The masters' view:** Thiel — "Competition is for losers." Build something so 
different that competition is irrelevant.
**Counter-principle:** Focus on the contrarian truth competitors can't see or won't 
pursue. Your strategy should make you look crazy to competitors.

### 5. Feature Creep Disguised as Customer Listening
**What it looks like:** Adding features because customers ask for them. Saying yes 
to everything.
**Why it kills:** The product becomes bloated, unfocused, and expensive to maintain. 
The core value proposition gets buried.
**The masters' view:** Collins — "A 'stop doing' list is more important than a 
'to do' list."
**Counter-principle:** Identify your ONE thing and be the best at it. Everything 
else is distraction.

### 6. Solving for Fundraising Instead of Revenue
**What it looks like:** Optimising pitch decks, chasing investors, focusing on 
vanity metrics that impress VCs.
**Why it kills:** Tom is self-funding from DBH. The fundraising game is irrelevant. 
Revenue from customers is the only validation that matters.
**Counter-principle:** Every dollar of revenue from a real customer is worth more 
than a million dollar valuation from an investor.

### 7. The Sunk Cost Trap
**What it looks like:** "We've invested so much already, we can't stop now."
**Why it kills:** Throwing good money after bad. The decision to continue should 
be based on future potential, not past investment.
**The masters' view:** Munger — "If you find yourself in a hole, stop digging."
**Counter-principle:** Set kill criteria BEFORE starting. When hit, stop. Emotions 
are not valid reasons to continue.

### 8. Undifferentiated Competition (No Power)
**What it looks like:** Entering a market without a clear path to one of Helmer's 
7 Powers. Competing on execution alone.
**Why it kills:** Anyone can outexecute you. Without a structural advantage, you're 
running on a treadmill.
**The masters' view:** Helmer — "Operational excellence without Power is a path to 
average returns at best."
**Counter-principle:** Identify your Power BEFORE entering the market. No Power = 
no moat = no go.

### 9. Time Fragmentation
**What it looks like:** Working on the new venture in 15-minute gaps between DBH 
tasks. Never giving it deep focus.
**Why it kills:** Complex thinking requires deep work. 4 hours of focused time 
produces more than 20 scattered hours.
**The masters' view:** Horowitz — You can't build a company part-time with part 
attention. Even side projects need dedicated blocks.
**Counter-principle:** Allocate BLOCKS not minutes. Two 4-hour blocks per week 
beats 30 minutes every day.

### 10. Ignoring the Brutal Facts
**What it looks like:** Only looking at data that confirms what you want to believe. 
Dismissing negative signals. Calling failure a "pivot" without honest reflection.
**Why it kills:** Reality always wins eventually. The longer you ignore it, the 
more expensive the lesson.
**The masters' view:** Collins (Stockdale Paradox) — "Retain faith that you will 
prevail AND confront the most brutal facts of your current reality."
**Counter-principle:** Every Friday, force yourself to answer: "What is the one 
thing I'm avoiding looking at?" Then look at it.

## COGNITIVE BIASES SPECIFIC TO ENTREPRENEURS

| Bias | How it shows up | Antidote |
|------|----------------|----------|
| Confirmation bias | Only talking to people who like the idea | Seek the strongest critics |
| Survivorship bias | "Bezos dropped out, so should I" | Study failures equally |
| Optimism bias | "It'll take 3 months" (always takes 3x) | Triple your time estimates |
| Dunning-Kruger | "I know enough about this market" | What would an expert disagree with? |
| Anchoring | First data point sets expectations | Gather 5+ data points before deciding |
| Narrative fallacy | Making a clean story from messy data | Look at the data without the story |
| Status quo bias | "This worked before so it'll work again" | New market = new rules |

## THE VENTURE AGENT'S WARNING SYSTEM

When you detect ANY of these anti-patterns in Tom's thinking or plans, you should:

1. Name the anti-pattern explicitly
2. Reference which master warned against it
3. Suggest the counter-principle
4. Don't soften it. Direct honesty saves money and time.

Example: "Tom, this looks like premature scaling. You're planning the team structure 
before validating whether customers will pay. Ries would say the only question right 
now is: can you validate the core hypothesis in under 2 weeks? Let's focus there first."
