# Strategic Frameworks for PREP

## 1. Bezos: Working Backwards
Start with the customer/end-state and work backwards to today.
- Write the press release first (what does the outcome look like?)
- Then work out what needs to be true for that to happen
- Then work out what to do this quarter, this month, this week
- "Start with the customer and work backwards" -- not "start with what we have"

Apply to Tom: When he has a vision, don't validate the vision first.
Ask: "What does the customer/user experience look like on day 1?
What needs to be true? What's the cheapest way to test that?"

## 2. Bezos: Two-Way Door Decisions
- One-way doors (irreversible): Think carefully. Take time.
- Two-way doors (reversible): Decide fast. Act. Adjust.
- Most decisions are two-way doors but people treat them as one-way.

Apply to Tom: He often agonises over decisions that are reversible.
"Is this a one-way or two-way door? If two-way, just do it and see."

## 3. Bezos: Customer Obsession
- Don't focus on competitors. Focus on what the customer needs.
- "Your margin is my opportunity" -- look for where others overcharge.
- Day 1 mentality: Always act like a startup, never get complacent.

Apply to Tom: Triple Whale charges $429/month for data he already owns.
This IS the opportunity. Build it yourself.

## 4. Dalio: Radical Transparency + Principles
- Document your principles (decision rules) from experience.
- When facing a decision, check: "Do I have a principle for this?"
- If yes, follow it. If no, think carefully, then create the principle.
- Radical transparency: Say what you actually think, not what's comfortable.

Apply to Tom: Track decisions and outcomes. Build Tom's personal principles
document over time. When he faces similar situations, reference them.

## 5. Dalio: The Machine
- See your business/life as a machine that produces outcomes.
- When outcomes don't match goals, diagnose the machine, don't just push harder.
- "Are the right people doing the right things to produce the right outcomes?"
- Design -> Operate -> Diagnose -> Redesign (loop)

Apply to Tom: When DBH isn't performing, don't just "work harder."
Diagnose: Is the channel wrong? The product? The targeting? The creative?

## 6. Dalio: Pain + Reflection = Progress
- Pain is a signal. Don't avoid it. Reflect on it.
- "The biggest mistake people make is not seeing their mistakes."
- Use setbacks to improve the machine.

Apply to Tom: His entire journey proves this. Bitcoin crash -> learned about risk.
London debt -> learned about relationships. Vietnam isolation -> found email marketing.
Junction AI failure -> found authentic alignment. Every crisis opened a door.

## 7. Jobs: Taste and Simplicity
- "Design is not just what it looks like. Design is how it works."
- Ruthlessly simplify. Say no to 1000 things to say yes to one.
- The intersection of technology and liberal arts.
- "Focus is about saying no."

Apply to Tom: He has too many irons in the fire. PREP should help him
decide what to say NO to, not just what to say yes to.

## 8. Jobs: Reality Distortion Field (Used Carefully)
- The ability to convince yourself and others that the impossible is possible.
- Useful for motivation and vision. Dangerous for self-deception.

Apply to Tom: He's experienced both sides. "Largest email company on the planet"
was distortion gone wrong. The Curiosity App vision felt real because it was
authentic. Test distortion against authenticity.

## 9. First Principles Thinking (Musk)
- Don't reason by analogy ("everyone else does it this way").
- Break the problem down to fundamental truths and build up from there.
- "What are we really trying to solve? What's physically possible?"

Apply to Tom: Triple Whale is reasoning by analogy (attribution needs a SaaS).
First principles: We have the raw data. We need to classify it. Build it ourselves.

## 10. The Eisenhower Matrix
- Urgent + Important = Do now
- Important + Not urgent = Schedule (most strategic work lives here)
- Urgent + Not important = Delegate or automate
- Not urgent + Not important = Eliminate

Apply to Tom: He often gets pulled into urgent-but-not-important work.
PREP should constantly ask: "Is this actually important, or just loud?"

## 11. Opportunity Cost Thinking
- Every hour spent on X is an hour NOT spent on Y.
- At Tom's income level (~$50/hr base), every decision has a dollar value.
- "What's the highest-leverage use of the next 4 hours?"

Apply to Tom: If he can earn $50/hr at DBH, spending 2 hours on a task worth
$20/hr is a net loss. Automate, delegate, or eliminate the $20/hr work.

## 12. The Regret Minimisation Framework (Bezos)
- "When I'm 80 years old, will I regret not trying this?"
- Used by Bezos to decide to leave Wall Street and start Amazon.
- Cuts through analysis paralysis for big life decisions.

Apply to Tom: Healthcare pivot, UK move, approaching KP Doyle --
these are the kind of decisions this framework is designed for.
"Will 80-year-old Tom regret not making that call?"
