## 1. DR PETER ATTIA — Longevity & The Medicine of Healthspan

**Physician specialising in the applied science of longevity. Author of "Outlive." 
The most rigorous thinker on how to live longer AND better.**

**Core principle:** The goal isn't lifespan (years alive) — it's healthspan (years 
of full physical and cognitive function). The last decade of life is determined by 
what you do in the decades before it.

**The "Centenarian Decathlon" concept:**
List the 10 physical tasks you want to be able to do at age 100 (or 80, or 90):
- Pick up a grandchild from the floor
- Walk up a flight of stairs with groceries
- Get off the floor unassisted
- Carry a suitcase overhead into an overhead bin

Then reverse-engineer: what level of strength, stability, cardio, and flexibility 
do you need NOW to still do those things at that age? Train for that.

**Attia's four pillars of longevity:**
1. **Exercise** — The single most powerful longevity intervention available. More 
   impactful than any drug or supplement. Non-negotiable.
2. **Nutrition** — Not about diet religion. About metabolic health: insulin sensitivity, 
   body composition, and avoiding metabolic syndrome.
3. **Sleep** — 7-9 hours. Non-negotiable. Performance, recovery, and cognitive function 
   all collapse without it.
4. **Emotional health** — Relationships and psychological wellbeing directly affect 
   mortality. Loneliness kills faster than obesity.

**Attia's exercise framework:**
- Zone 2 cardio: 150-180 minutes/week (the foundation — this is where most people 
  under-invest). Zone 2 = can hold a conversation but it's uncomfortable.
- VO2 max training: 1-2 sessions/week of high-intensity intervals. VO2 max is the 
  single strongest predictor of all-cause mortality.
- Strength training: 3-4 sessions/week. Muscle mass is the "bank account" you draw 
  from as you age. You can't have too much.
- Stability: Daily mobility work. Injury prevention. The thing that keeps you training.

**For Tom specifically:**
- BJJ provides excellent Zone 2 AND VO2 max training
- But it doesn't provide systematic strength training — that needs to be supplemented
- Injury prevention is CRITICAL for BJJ longevity (stability training)
- The "athlete's paradox": train hard enough to grow, not so hard you can't recover

**When Titan should invoke Attia:** Any programming decision, recovery planning, 
long-term health strategy.

## 2. DR ANDY GALPIN — Exercise Science & Programming

**Professor of Kinesiology. Co-host of the Huberman Lab exercise series. The most 
accessible translator of exercise science into practical programming.**

**The 9 Physical Adaptations (Galpin's framework):**
Every training program should intentionally develop some subset of:
1. Skill (movement quality, technique)
2. Speed (rate of force development)
3. Power (speed × strength)
4. Strength (maximal force production)
5. Hypertrophy (muscle size)
6. Muscular endurance (repeat efforts)
7. Anaerobic capacity (short intense efforts, 30-120 seconds)
8. VO2 max (maximal oxygen utilisation)
9. Long-duration endurance (sustained effort)

**For BJJ athletes, priority order:**
1. Skill (#1, always — mat time)
2. Muscular endurance (#6 — grip endurance, sustained grappling)
3. Anaerobic capacity (#7 — explosive scrambles, single-round intensity)
4. Strength (#4 — frame strength, takedown resistance)
5. VO2 max (#8 — recovery between rounds)
6. Hypertrophy (#5 — within weight class, functional mass)

**Galpin's programming principles:**
- **Specificity:** Train for what you need. BJJ demands grip endurance and isometric 
  strength more than bodybuilding-style hypertrophy.
- **Progressive overload:** Gradual increase in stimulus. Not random "hard" workouts.
- **Recovery is the adaptation:** You don't grow in the gym. You grow recovering FROM 
  the gym. Sleep, nutrition, and rest days ARE the program.
- **Minimum effective dose:** The least amount of training that produces the adaptation 
  you want. More is not always better.
- **Periodisation:** You can't train everything maximally at once. Block or undulating 
  periodisation cycles different qualities.

**When Titan should invoke Galpin:** Program design, exercise selection, understanding 
why a particular training approach is recommended.

## 3. JOHN DANAHER — BJJ Strategy & Systems Thinking

**Widely considered the greatest BJJ coach in history. His students dominate at the 
highest levels. His approach is pure systems thinking applied to grappling.**

**Core principles:**

- **Position before submission:** Control the position first. Submissions come from 
  positional dominance, not from scrambles. The hierarchy: takedown → pass → control 
  → submit.

- **Systems over techniques:** Don't learn 500 disconnected techniques. Learn 5-6 
  interconnected systems where each move creates dilemmas that lead to the next. 
  "Enter the system" is the goal — once in, the opponent faces branching decisions 
  where every choice leads to a worse position.

- **Dilemma-based attacks:** Never give the opponent a single problem to solve. Give 
  them TWO problems simultaneously where defending one exposes them to the other. 
  The opponent can solve A or B, but not both.

- **Efficiency over athleticism:** Technique multiplies athleticism. A 5 in technique 
  with a 5 in athleticism (25) beats a 3 in technique with a 9 in athleticism (27 is 
  close, but the technical fighter improves faster and ages better).

- **The power of the study:** Film review, conceptual analysis, and deliberate 
  practice off the mat accelerate development more than just rolling more rounds.

**Danaher's hierarchy of grappling positions (from most to least dominant):**
1. Rear mount (back control with hooks/body triangle)
2. Mount
3. Knee on belly
4. Side control
5. Half guard (top)
6. Guard (various — playing guard is neutral, not losing)
7. Turtle (defensive, vulnerable)
8. Under side control
9. Under mount
10. Giving up the back (worst position)

**For Tom's training:**
- Each training session should have a conceptual focus (not just "rolling")
- Film review of own competition matches identifies pattern weaknesses
- Drilling should be system-based: "Today I'm working entries to the leg lock system"
- Positional sparring > free rolling for skill development (more reps, specific learning)

## 4. DR LAYNE NORTON — Nutrition Science & Flexible Dieting

**PhD in Nutritional Sciences. Competitive powerlifter and bodybuilder. The most 
evidence-based voice in nutrition, famous for dismantling nutrition myths.**

**Core principles:**

- **Energy balance is king:** Weight gain or loss is fundamentally about calories in 
  vs calories out. No food is magic. No food is poison. Context and quantity matter.

- **Protein is the priority macro:** For active individuals: 1.6-2.2g per kg body 
  weight per day. Spread across 4+ meals for optimal muscle protein synthesis. 
  This is the one macro worth tracking precisely.

- **Flexible dieting (IIFYM done right):** No foods are "off limits." Hitting your 
  calorie and protein targets while eating mostly whole foods (80/20 rule) is 
  sustainable long-term. Rigid diets lead to restriction→binge cycles.

- **Diet adherence > diet composition:** The best diet is the one you can follow 
  consistently. A "perfect" diet you follow for 3 weeks then abandon is worse than 
  a "good enough" diet you follow for years.

- **Rate of weight change matters:** 
  - Cutting: 0.5-1% body weight per week (preserves muscle)
  - Bulking: 0.25-0.5% body weight per week (minimises fat gain)
  - Faster than this = losing muscle or gaining unnecessary fat

**Norton's supplement hierarchy (what actually works):**
1. Creatine monohydrate (5g/day — strongest evidence base of any supplement)
2. Caffeine (performance enhancer, well-studied)
3. Protein supplementation (if whole food intake is insufficient)
4. Vitamin D (if deficient — common in NZ winters)
5. Omega-3s / fish oil (anti-inflammatory — and this is where GLM fits)
6. Everything else: marginal at best

**For Tom's nutrition:**
- Track calories and protein. Don't stress about exact carb/fat split.
- Pre-competition: Gradual cut (0.5-0.75% BW/week) starting 8-12 weeks out
- Post-competition: Reverse diet back up (don't binge)
- BJJ training days: Higher carb, higher calorie
- Rest days: Slightly lower calorie, maintain protein

## 5. DR ANDREW HUBERMAN — Neuroscience of Performance

**Stanford neuroscientist. The bridge between brain science and practical performance 
optimisation.**

**Key protocols for athletic performance:**

- **Morning sunlight (10-30 min):** Sets circadian rhythm. Improves sleep quality 
  that night. Free performance enhancer.
- **Cold exposure (deliberate):** 1-3 min cold water (11°C or colder) increases 
  dopamine 2.5x for hours. BUT: don't use immediately after strength training 
  (blunts hypertrophy signal). Use on non-training days or before skill work.
- **Heat exposure (sauna):** 20 min at 80-100°C, 2-3x/week. Growth hormone release, 
  cardiovascular benefits. Good for BJJ recovery days.
- **Non-sleep deep rest (NSDR):** 10-20 min yoga nidra or body scan between training 
  sessions. Accelerates learning and recovery without full nap.
- **Focus protocols:** 90-min work blocks match natural ultradian cycles. Deep focus 
  for 90 min, then genuine rest. Applies to training sessions too.

**Huberman on learning motor skills (BJJ application):**
- Errors drive learning. Making mistakes in training literally creates the neural 
  signal for improvement.
- Repetitions in the first 24 hours after learning a new technique determine retention.
- Sleep is where motor learning consolidates. A new technique drilled Tuesday is 
  "installed" Tuesday night's sleep.
- Mental rehearsal (visualisation) activates 60-80% of the same neural pathways as 
  physical practice. Useful before competition and on rest days.

## 6. PAVEL TSATSOULINE — Strength Without Size

**Former Soviet special forces instructor. Brought kettlebell training and "greasing 
the groove" to the West. Master of getting strong without getting big — perfect for 
combat athletes who need strength within a weight class.**

**Core principles:**

- **Strength is a skill:** Treat strength training like skill practice. Fresh, focused, 
  submaximal. Never train to failure on main lifts. Leave 2-3 reps in reserve.
- **Greasing the groove:** Do many sets of low reps throughout the day (never to failure). 
  The nervous system adapts without fatigue. Perfect for pullups, pushups, grip work.
- **The minimum effective dose:** Train with the least volume that produces adaptation. 
  More volume = more fatigue = less mat time. For BJJ athletes, 2-3 strength sessions 
  per week of 30-45 minutes each is plenty.
- **"Same but different":** Wave loading and variation prevent staleness without 
  abandoning the movement pattern. Heavy day → medium day → light day → repeat.
- **Tension techniques:** Learn to create maximal full-body tension for strength 
  expression. Grip harder, brace harder, breathe properly. "Tension = force."

**Pavel's programming for combat athletes:**
- Deadlifts, presses, pullups as main lifts (simple, compound, functional)
- Kettlebell swings and Turkish get-ups for conditioning and stability
- Keep sessions short and intense (30-45 min)
- Never compromise recovery — mat time is the priority

## SYNTHESIS: THE TITAN PROGRAMMING STACK

### Weekly structure principles:
1. **BJJ is the #1 priority** — Everything else supports mat time (Danaher)
2. **Strength serves grappling** — 2-3 sessions, submaximal, no failure (Pavel)
3. **Zone 2 is non-negotiable** — 150+ minutes/week minimum (Attia)
4. **Protein is the priority macro** — 1.6-2.2g/kg/day across 4+ meals (Norton)
5. **Sleep is where you improve** — 7-9 hours, non-negotiable (Huberman/Attia)
6. **Recovery IS the program** — You grow outside the gym (Galpin)

### Decision hierarchy for Tom:
- Injured → Rest. No exceptions. (Attia: healthspan > short-term performance)
- Competition prep → Prioritise mat time + skill work (Danaher: position before submission)
- Building phase → Add strength volume (Galpin: progressive overload)
- Cutting weight → Slow and steady, protein high (Norton: 0.5-1% BW/week)
- Low energy → Check sleep first, then nutrition, then training volume (Huberman)
