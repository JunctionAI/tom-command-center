## VAGAL TONING TECHNIQUES (Phase 1+)

**1. Slow Exhale Breathing (Primary — use daily)**
- Inhale through nose for 4 seconds
- Exhale through mouth for 6-8 seconds (longer exhale is key)
- The extended exhale activates the parasympathetic nervous system
- Do for 2-5 minutes
- IMPORTANT for Jackson: Do NOT focus on "controlling" the breath. Frame as "inviting a slower exhale" not "breathing exercise." Given his breath hyperawareness, the language matters.

**2. Humming**
- Take a normal breath in
- Hum on the exhale — any note, any tune
- The vibration stimulates the vagus nerve directly in the throat
- 5-10 hums is enough
- Can do while walking, cooking, or lying down
- Low-effort, high-impact for Jackson's fatigue level

**3. Cold Face Immersion (Dive Reflex)**
- Fill a bowl with cold water
- Submerge face for 15-30 seconds
- OR: splash cold water on face, focusing on forehead and cheeks
- OR: hold a cold pack against forehead and cheeks
- Activates the mammalian dive reflex → instant parasympathetic activation
- Particularly useful during anxiety spikes

**4. Gargling**
- Gargle water vigorously for 30 seconds
- The back of the throat is vagal nerve territory
- Simple and can be done multiple times daily
- Good for Jackson because it also gently activates the swallowing muscles (helpful for dysphagia)

**5. Singing or Chanting**
- Any song works — the vocal cord vibration is the mechanism
- Even quiet humming of a tune counts
- Combines vagal stimulation with emotional regulation
- More social versions: singing along with music

## SAFETY REAPPRAISAL SCRIPTS (Phase 1-2)

Use when Jackson's fear-of-damage thoughts spike. He can say these internally or aloud.

**When tension/pressure increases:**
"I notice the tension. My nervous system is being protective. This is not damage occurring. Chronic tension is functional — it creates real sensations but does not damage nerves or muscles. I am safe right now."

**When symptoms feel overwhelming:**
"My alarm system is very active right now. That makes sense given what my nervous system has been through. These sensations are intense but not dangerous. They will fluctuate. I don't need to fix them right now."

**When fear spirals about permanent damage:**
"Years of medical evidence shows that stress-related tension does not cause permanent nerve damage. What I'm feeling is my nervous system doing what it learned to do. It can unlearn this. I am not being damaged right now."

**When eating triggers symptoms:**
"Chewing and swallowing require neuromuscular effort. My nervous system interprets this effort as threatening because it's on high alert. The difficulty is real but it is functional — my swallowing muscles are not being damaged. Liquid nutrition is a valid choice that reduces this stress."

**When symptoms spike with screens:**
"Screen use increases cognitive load, which my sensitised nervous system interprets as threat. The pressure I feel is a stress response, not increased cranial pressure. I can take a break, and the sensation will settle."

## SOMATIC TRACKING PROTOCOL (Phase 2+)
From Alan Gordon's Pain Reprocessing Therapy.

**Step 1: Choose a symptom to track**
Pick one — tension, pressure, tingling, whatever is present right now.

**Step 2: Observe with curiosity, not fear**
"What does it actually feel like? Is it sharp or dull? Is it constant or does it pulse? Does it have a shape or size? Is it moving or still?"

**Step 3: Bring a sense of safety**
While observing: "This sensation is my nervous system's signal. It is not evidence of damage. I am safe while I observe this."

**Step 4: Notice any changes**
Often, just observing with safety reduces intensity. If it increases — that's okay too. The goal is not to change the sensation, just to change your relationship to it.

**Step 5: Return to activity**
After 1-2 minutes of tracking, gently return attention to whatever you were doing. You don't need to fix it.

**Key instruction for Jackson:** The success metric is NOT whether the symptom reduced. It's whether you observed it without adding fear. That IS the win.

## PENDULATION EXERCISE (Phase 2+)
From Peter Levine's Somatic Experiencing.

**Step 1: Find a resource**
"Where in your body do you feel most at ease right now? Maybe your hands, your feet, your lower back. Find one spot that feels neutral or comfortable."

**Step 2: Rest in the resource**
Spend 30 seconds with attention gently on that comfortable place. Notice what it feels like. Warmth? Stillness? Softness?

**Step 3: Gently move to discomfort**
"Now slowly bring your attention to a place that feels tense or uncomfortable. Don't dive in — just approach the edge."

**Step 4: Notice the sensation briefly**
5-10 seconds. What does it feel like? Don't try to change it.

**Step 5: Return to the resource**
Move attention back to the comfortable spot. Rest there again.

**Step 6: Repeat 2-3 times**
Each cycle, you can stay a moment longer with the discomfort if it feels manageable. Never push.

**Why this works for Jackson:** It teaches his nervous system that discomfort is not permanent — it can move back to comfort. This is the opposite of his current pattern (attention locks onto discomfort and stays there).

## GROUNDING: 5-4-3-2-1 SENSORY (Phase 1+)

When anxiety spikes or dissociation begins:
- **5** things you can SEE (name them specifically)
- **4** things you can TOUCH (feel the texture)
- **3** things you can HEAR (listen for quiet sounds)
- **2** things you can SMELL (or imagine smelling)
- **1** thing you can TASTE (or take a sip of water)

This shifts attention from internal monitoring to external sensory reality. Particularly useful for Jackson when he's trapped in the symptom-monitoring loop.

## RF-ERP FOR BREATH AWARENESS (Phase 2+)

**The framework (from Dr Michael Greenberg):**

1. **Notice:** "I'm aware of my breathing right now."
2. **Label:** "This is my sensorimotor OCD pattern activating."
3. **Don't engage:** The urge will come to think: "Why can't I stop? Will this ever go away? What if I always notice?" DO NOT follow these thoughts. They are the compulsion.
4. **Redirect:** Gently return attention to whatever you were doing. It's okay if awareness of breathing continues. The goal is not stopping the awareness — it's stopping the REACTION to the awareness.
5. **Accept:** "The awareness is here. That's okay. It's not dangerous. I don't need to fix it right now."

**What counts as progress:**
- Reduced DISTRESS about the awareness (even if awareness continues)
- Shorter time spent ruminating after noticing
- More moments in the day where you forget to monitor
- The fact that Jackson's breath hyperawareness has already "slowly gotten better" IS evidence this works

**What Jackson should NOT do:**
- Try to "not notice" breathing (paradoxical — trying not to notice increases noticing)
- Deep breathing exercises that draw attention TO the breath (counterproductive for sensorimotor OCD)
- Breathing exercises where the instruction is "notice your breath" (use exhale-focused vagal toning instead, which is about the LENGTH of exhale, not awareness of breath)

## PROGRESSIVE MUSCLE RELAXATION — MODIFIED (Phase 1+)

Standard PMR involves tensing then relaxing muscles. For Jackson, we SKIP the tensing (his muscles are already chronically tense). Instead:

**Release-only PMR:**
1. Lie down or sit comfortably
2. Start at feet: "Let your feet be heavy. Let them sink into the surface beneath you."
3. Move up: ankles, calves, knees, thighs — same instruction for each
4. Abdomen: "Let your belly be soft. There's nothing to hold here."
5. Chest: "Your chest can be open and quiet."
6. Hands, arms, shoulders: "Let your shoulders drop away from your ears. Arms heavy."
7. Neck: "Your neck doesn't need to hold anything right now."
8. Jaw: "Let your jaw soften. Tongue resting gently."
9. Face: "Smooth your forehead. Let your eyes be soft behind closed lids."
10. Whole body: "Everything is supported. Nothing needs to be held."

Time: 5-10 minutes. Good for evening wind-down (6:30pm check-in suggestion).
