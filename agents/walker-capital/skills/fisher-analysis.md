# Skill: Fisher Analysis — 15-Point Qualitative Framework
## Vesper — Walker Capital Management

Philip Fisher's "Scuttlebutt" method — the qualitative anchor on every decision memo.
Scored 1–5 per point. Maximum score: 75. Report total and flag top strengths + top concerns.

---

## THE 15 POINTS

**1. Products/Services with sufficient market potential (1–5)**
Does the company have products or services with sufficient market potential to make possible a sizable increase in sales for at least several years? Or is it locked into a market with natural ceiling?
Score 5: Large, growing TAM with clear runway. Company capturing share.
Score 1: Saturated market, no growth vector, product in structural decline.

**2. Management's determination to develop new products (1–5)**
Is management determined to continue developing products or services to further increase total sales when growth potential of current lines is exploited? Innovation pipeline depth.
Score 5: Consistent R&D investment, clear product roadmap, track record of successful new launches.
Score 1: No R&D, milking existing products, no visible pipeline.

**3. R&D effectiveness relative to size (1–5)**
How effective is the company's R&D effort? Revenue generated per R&D dollar. Quality of R&D output, not just quantity of spend.
Score 5: High R&D yield, proprietary technology, defensible IP.
Score 1: High R&D spend with poor commercialisation track record.

**4. Above-average sales organisation (1–5)**
Does the company have a sales organisation that understands customers deeply and can win? Quality of go-to-market, customer relationships, retention rates, NPS.
Score 5: Market-leading retention, strong referral network, pricing power demonstrated.
Score 1: High churn, commoditised selling, price-only competition.

**5. Worthwhile profit margin (1–5)**
Does the company have a worthwhile profit margin? Not just current margin but trajectory and durability. Pricing power + operating leverage.
Score 5: Expanding margins with clear operating leverage mechanism. Top-quartile vs peers.
Score 1: Thin/declining margins, no pricing power, commoditised.

**6. What is being done to maintain/improve profit margins (1–5)**
Is management actively managing the cost structure? Automation, scale benefits, mix shift toward higher-margin products? Specific initiatives that will show up in numbers.
Score 5: Specific margin improvement programmes with measurable milestones and track record of delivery.
Score 1: No cost discipline, passive on margins, blaming external factors.

**7. Outstanding labour and personnel relations (1–5)**
Does the company treat its employees well? Evidence: low turnover, Glassdoor ratings, management accessibility, culture of development. Poor relations = operational risk and talent drain.
Score 5: Low turnover, strong Glassdoor/employer brand, management invests in people.
Score 1: High turnover, litigation history, toxic culture indicators.

**8. Outstanding executive relations (1–5)**
Do executives across the organisation work well together? No single point of failure (over-dependence on one person). Depth of management bench. Low executive turnover.
Score 5: Deep bench, collaborative culture, executives stay long-term, clear succession.
Score 1: Key-person risk, high executive turnover, internal conflicts visible.

**9. Management depth (1–5)**
Does the company have depth to its management? Can it promote from within? Is the next layer ready? Especially important as the company scales.
Score 5: Strong middle management, internal promotion track record, talent development programme.
Score 1: Thin management below C-suite, depends on founders for everything.

**10. Cost analysis and accounting controls (1–5)**
Does management have good cost visibility and controls? Can they tell within a reasonable time if costs are getting out of control? Quality of management accounting.
Score 5: Real-time cost dashboards, strong CFO, clean audit history, no restatements.
Score 1: Opaque cost structure, restatements, weak CFO, audit qualifications.

**11. Industry-specific factors (1–5)**
Are there factors peculiar to this industry that give important clues about the investment? Regulatory environment, licensing requirements, switching costs, network effects, platform dynamics. Industry-specific moat sources.
Score 5: Structural industry tailwinds, regulatory protection, high switching costs.
Score 1: Industry headwinds, regulatory risk, commoditising forces.

**12. Short-range vs long-range outlook (1–5)**
Does management take a genuinely long-term view? Do they sacrifice short-term profits for long-term positioning? Or do they manage to quarterly EPS guidance at the expense of the business?
Score 5: Long-term investment orientation, willing to invest through cycles, consistent communication.
Score 1: Manages to quarterly numbers, cuts R&D/marketing when under pressure, short-termist.

**13. Equity financing dilution risk (1–5)**
Will the growth of the company require so much equity financing that the resulting dilution will largely offset the gains? Important for capital-intensive or high-burn growth companies.
Score 5: Self-funding growth, no equity dilution required, strong FCF generation funding investment.
Score 1: Ongoing dilution required to fund growth, capital intensity high, equity raises frequent.

**14. Management transparency during difficulties (1–5)**
Does management talk freely to investors when things are going well but clam up when trouble occurs? Transparency under pressure is a character test.
Score 5: Consistent, candid communication in good times and bad. Owns mistakes clearly.
Score 1: Promotional in good times, vague/defensive when results disappoint.

**15. Management integrity (1–5)**
Unquestionable integrity: shareholder alignment, no related-party abuse, appropriate compensation, honest accounting, consistent stated vs actual priorities.
Score 5: Management buys stock with own money, aligned compensation, no related-party concerns.
Score 1: Related party transactions, excessive comp vs performance, accounting aggression.

---

## SCORING GUIDE

75–65: Exceptional management and business quality. Rare. Strong qualitative support.
64–50: Good. Above average. Minor concerns but overall sound.
49–35: Average. Some structural concerns. Risk-weighted in conviction score.
34–20: Below average. Qualitative concerns are material to thesis.
< 20: Significant qualitative red flags. Requires explicit discussion to proceed.

---

## HOW TO RESEARCH EACH POINT
- Annual reports and shareholder letters (management voice)
- Earnings call transcripts (consistency of narrative vs results)
- Glassdoor and LinkedIn (employee sentiment, turnover signals)
- Industry analyst reports and customer reviews
- Regulatory filings (related party, executive comp disclosures)
- News archives (how management behaved in the last crisis)
- Morningstar stewardship rating (Exemplary / Standard / Poor) as a calibration check
