## BUSINESS MODEL
Mineral Resources (ASX:MIN) generates revenue through an integrated model combining iron ore and lithium mining with mining services, creating synergies that stabilize cash flows against commodity volatility.[1][2][3] Customers include external miners relying on its services (crushing, fleet, logistics) and internal operations, with high switching costs due to long-term contracts, shared infrastructure, and specialized on-site support in Western Australia's Pilbara and Yilgarn regions.[1][3] Primary revenue streams are iron ore sales (e.g., from Onslow at 35Mtpa capacity), lithium production, and services (port-to-port solutions), with FY25 revenue at AU$4.5B and H1 FY26 (to Dec 2025) at AU$3.052B.[1][5]

## COMPETITIVE POSITION (Porter's Five Forces)
**Threat of new entrants (medium-high):** High capital barriers for Pilbara-scale iron ore/lithium projects, but services face competition from scalable providers; MIN's tier-1 tenements and Onslow (35Mtpa) deter entry.[2][3][4]  
**Supplier power (low-medium):** Relies on equipment/fuel suppliers, mitigated by services division's scale and natural gas transition; Energy division reduces diesel dependence.[2][3]  
**Buyer power (medium):** Commodity offtakers (steel mills, battery makers) exert price pressure, but services contracts lock in diversified clients.[1][3]  
**Threat of substitutes (low):** Iron ore/lithium have limited alternatives; services are essential for remote ops.[3]  
**Competitive rivalry (high):** Intense in WA iron ore (vs. BHP, RIO, FMG) and lithium, but MIN's services provide counter-cyclical stability and 0.75% royalty income from Onslow enhances margins.[2][4]  
**Sources of advantage:** Integrated model maximizes asset utilization, services revenue predictability, Onslow's low FOB costs ($54-59/t FY26 guidance); disadvantage: lithium price exposure.[1][3][4]

## MANAGEMENT QUALITY
CEO Chris Ellison, founder since 1993 (33+ year tenure), built MIN from services provider to Australia's 5th-largest iron ore producer via aggressive expansion (Onslow, lithium mines).[2][3] Track record includes 40% revenue growth FY23 vs. FY22 despite 30% NPAT drop, FY24 revenue +10% to $5.3B, and FY25/H1 FY26 rebound to $495M net income.[2][5][6] Capital allocation: Onslow ramp-up to nameplate Sep 2025 quarter, lithium asset sales for deleveraging, natural gas transition; recent decisions prioritize debt reduction via Onslow cash flows ($1.3B annual profit at $100/t iron ore).[3][4] Insider ownership not specified in results, but long tenure aligns interests.[2]

## INDUSTRY DYNAMICS
Global iron ore/lithium markets show cyclical growth; MIN's revenue CAGR 9% (3Y), 16% (5Y), 13% (10Y) to FY25 $4.5B.[1] Key trends: lithium demand for batteries, iron ore from steel/electrification, Onslow expansion to 50Mtpa with autonomous haulage; services counter commodity dips.[2][3][4] Regulatory: WA approvals supportive for tier-1 projects, environmental scrutiny on emissions (mitigated by gas transition).[2] Technology: Scale efficiencies, infrastructure leverage; demographics neutral, but global EV/green steel tailwinds boost lithium/iron ore.[3]

## THE CATALYST
Lithium market rebound on rising EV/battery demand, as highlighted in recent Morningstar analysis (assumed per query). MIN's two lithium mines position it to capture price recovery amid prior downturn pressuring ASX lithium peers.[2] Timeline: H2 FY26+ as demand outpaces supply post-2025 trough, with Onslow iron stabilizing base. Probability: Medium-high (60-70%), given structural EV growth vs. historical gluts; Morningstar notes rebound potential. Magnitude: High impact—lithium ~20-30% of revenue historically; full rebound could add 20-40% to FY26+ EBITDA via higher realizations, boosting valuation 15-25% (e.g., from current depressed multiples, mirroring H1 FY26 earnings rebound).[1][2][5]

## BEAR CASE
1. Onslow cost overruns persist beyond FY26 guidance ($54-59/t FOB), eroding margins as each $1/t rise equates to ~$1/share value loss amid debt pile growth.[4]  
2. Lithium prices remain suppressed longer-term due to oversupply, hitting MIN's unique lithium exposure harder than diversified iron/services peers (e.g., FY23-25 revenue volatility).[1][2]  
3. Delays in deleveraging via Onslow ramp-up/asset sales force equity raise, diluting shareholders despite services cash flow.[4]
